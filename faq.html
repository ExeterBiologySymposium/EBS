General

EBS is a student-organized research symposium hosted by the Genetics and Biotech Club at Phillips Exeter Academy. It gives students with little or no prior research experience the opportunity to conduct, present, and discuss original dry-lab biological research alongside peers, mentors, and judges. The inaugural event is planned for Spring 2027.

Dry-lab research refers to research conducted without physical laboratory experiments or wet-lab techniques (pipettes, cell cultures, reagents, etc.). Instead, it uses computational analysis, publicly available datasets, literature synthesis, bioinformatics tools, and statistical modeling. Examples include genomics studies using publicly available sequence databases, meta-analyses of existing literature, disease modeling from epidemiological data, and computational protein structure prediction.

Dry-lab research is ideal for high school students because it requires a standard laptop rather than specialized lab equipment, and many powerful tools and datasets are freely accessible online.

EBS 2027 is planned for Spring 2027 — the target date is May 15, 2027, though this may be adjusted. The event will be held on the campus of Phillips Exeter Academy in Exeter, New Hampshire. The specific venue on campus will be confirmed and communicated to all registered participants in advance.

EBS is organized entirely by students in the Genetics and Biotech Club at Phillips Exeter Academy, with guidance from faculty advisors. The co-heads are Adrian Chan, Jaiden Kim, Reya Satam, and Luke Wang. Faculty oversight is provided by Summer Morrill (Biology Department, Phillips Exeter Academy). The organizing committee also includes working groups covering research curriculum, outreach, logistics, media, and abstract review. See the Team page for details.

EBS aims to be free for all student participants. There is no registration fee. Any travel to the symposium venue is the participant's own responsibility. We are actively seeking sponsors to provide financial support for students who need it. Volunteers (mentors, judges) participate at no cost.

For Students

No. EBS is specifically designed for students who have not done biological research before. The entire program — from our resource library to mentor matching — is built around helping beginners. If you have never read a scientific paper or used a research database, that is exactly the starting point EBS is built for.

EBS 2027 is open to any current student at Phillips Exeter Academy, regardless of grade or prior science coursework. We may expand to partner schools in future years. If you attend a different school and are interested, contact us — we're tracking demand for future expansion.

Most participants spend 2–4 hours per week from October through May. This includes research time, mentor check-ins, and poster/presentation preparation. The time commitment peaks in February (abstract deadline) and May (presentation prep). EBS is designed to fit alongside a full academic schedule.

Yes. Solo projects, pairs, and teams of up to three students are all accepted. Each team member must contribute meaningfully to the research and be prepared to discuss any part of the project during the poster session. All team members register individually.

Yes. EBS does not restrict students from submitting their work to other science fairs, competitions, or academic journals. We actively encourage students to share their research broadly. EBS is a launchpad, not an exclusive commitment.

Research & Projects

Any original dry-lab biological research is eligible. This includes:

  • Literature synthesis / meta-analysis: Review and synthesize research on a biological question
  • Bioinformatics: Analyze genomic, proteomic, or transcriptomic datasets using online tools
  • Computational modeling: Build or apply models to simulate biological systems
  • Epidemiological analysis: Analyze disease spread, risk factors, or public health data
  • Ecological data analysis: Study population dynamics, biodiversity, or habitat data using existing datasets
  • Product design with a biological application: Design a tool, device, or system that addresses a real biological or health problem — supported with research on the problem space, existing solutions, and your design rationale

The subject must be biology-adjacent — we interpret this broadly. If your project engages with a living system, human health, an ecosystem, or the interface between biology and technology, it almost certainly qualifies. When in doubt, reach out and we'll tell you.

Projects must use publicly available tools and data (no proprietary or gated resources that reviewers can't verify). Wet-lab experiments are not accepted at EBS.

Start with something you're genuinely curious about. Think about biology topics that came up in class, news stories that caught your attention, or questions you've always had. A good EBS research question is: specific (not "what causes cancer" but "what genetic mutations are most common in pediatric glioblastoma"), answerable with public data, and biological in nature.

Your assigned mentor will help you refine your question into something workable. See the Resources page for guides on forming research questions and finding datasets.

All tools used in EBS research are free and browser-accessible. Common tools include: PubMed and Google Scholar for literature; NCBI, UCSC Genome Browser, and UniProt for biological databases; Galaxy and Google Colab for bioinformatics; R/RStudio for statistical analysis. No coding experience is required — EBS provides beginner guides for all tools in the resource library.

Students submit a structured abstract in February 2027. Abstracts are reviewed by the EBS abstract review committee (composed of EBS organizing team members and volunteer reviewers). All students who submit a complete, original abstract in scope for EBS are expected to be accepted to present, though we may provide feedback for revision in borderline cases. Acceptance notifications go out in March 2027.

Mentors & Support

Yes. We aim to match every registered student with a volunteer researcher mentor who has expertise relevant to their research area. Matching takes place in October 2026. Mentors are matched based on stated research interests — if you haven't decided on a topic yet, that's okay, we'll do our best with preliminary interests.

Mentors meet with their matched students approximately twice per month via video call (~30 min each). They help students refine their research question, identify appropriate data sources and tools, review work in progress, give feedback on abstracts and posters, and help students prepare to talk about their work. Mentors are not expected to do the research for students — their role is to guide and advise.

EBS will provide all students with access to the resource library and organizing team support regardless of mentor availability. If a close match is unavailable, we will assign you a mentor with adjacent expertise who can still help with research fundamentals. We will communicate transparently if matching constraints arise.

Symposium Day

EBS offers two presentation formats:

  • Poster Presentation: A printed or digital research poster displayed during one or both poster sessions. Students stand by their poster and explain their work to visiting judges and attendees (~15 min per judge visit). This is the primary format and is available to all accepted participants.
  • Short Talk (Oral Presentation): A 10-minute slide presentation followed by a 5-minute Q&A. Available to a limited number of participants selected during abstract review.

Students may indicate their presentation format preference in the abstract submission. EBS will do its best to honor preferences, subject to scheduling constraints.

Poster presenters should bring their printed poster (size specifications will be provided). Oral presenters should bring a laptop with slides loaded (backup on USB recommended). Wear professional or business casual attire — you are presenting original research to professionals. All other materials will be provided at check-in.

Yes. Lunch will be provided for all registered participants, judges, and volunteers. Light refreshments will be available throughout the day. Please indicate any dietary restrictions when you register — we will do our best to accommodate them.

We are planning for a limited guest list given venue constraints. Details on guest attendance will be communicated to registered participants in early 2027. At minimum, we expect to allow one family member or support person per student presenter.

For Schools

EBS 2027 is focused on Phillips Exeter Academy students. Expanding to include partner schools is something we're actively considering for future years. If you are an educator at another school who is interested in a future partnership, please contact us — we'd love to hear from you.

Faculty advisors provide light oversight and institutional support — checking in on student progress, helping with any scheduling conflicts, and being a point of contact for the EBS organizing team. Faculty advisors are not expected to provide academic research mentorship (that's what our volunteer researchers do). This role typically requires under 1 hour per month.

For Judges

Judges should have at minimum an undergraduate degree in a biological science or related STEM field. Graduate students, postdoctoral researchers, faculty members, and industry professionals are all welcome. Judges should be comfortable evaluating research methodology and scientific communication at a high school level.

All judges receive a briefing packet before Symposium Day including: the judging rubric, abstract summaries of the projects they're assigned to evaluate, a venue map and schedule, and guidelines for providing constructive feedback to first-time student researchers. A brief virtual orientation is held the week before the event.

For EBS 2027, we are planning for in-person judging only. Live poster Q&A is core to the student experience, and remote evaluation would significantly reduce the quality of feedback students receive. We may explore hybrid options in future years.
